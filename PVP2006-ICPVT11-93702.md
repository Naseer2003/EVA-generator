EXTREME VALUE ANALYSIS OF HEAT EXCHANGER TUBE INSPECTION DATA 
 
 
W. David Wang, Ph.D. 
Shell Oil Products Company 
 
 
ABSTRACT  This paper presents a statistical extreme value 
analysis (EVA) method for assessment of the minimum 
remaining thickness of tubes in industrial heat exchangers, 
including air-cooled heat exchangers and shell-and-tube heat 
exchangers.  Five test cases are used to evaluate the 
effectiveness of the EVA method developed in this work.  In 
each case, real inspection data from heat exchangers in 
petroleum refinery and petrochemical plant are analyzed, and 
the results discussed. The effectiveness of the EVA method is 
then evaluated per the Inspection Effectiveness definition used 
by American Petroleum Institute (API) for Risk-Based 
Inspection (RBI), so that the EVA method and results can be 
easily incorporated in API RBI assessment.  It is found 
through these test cases that the most ideal sample size seems 
to be 20 – 30 tubes, regardless of the number of tubes in the 
heat exchanger.  At that sample size, A, B, C and D levels of 
API RBI inspection effectiveness can be achieved by using the 
lower bound value of the 99%, 95%, 90%, and 80% 
confidence intervals, respectively.   
 
INTRODUCTION 
 
Statistical extreme value analysis (EVA) has been used as a 
means to assess corrosion rates and remaining life in industrial 
applications.  Extensive work has been done and published by 
the Japan Society of Corrosion Engineering [1].  Use of EVA 
has also been standardized in ASTM E 2283 – 03 for 
assessment of the maximum size of inclusion in steels based 
on inclusion length measurements performed on only 24 
cross-sections [2].   
 
This paper presents an EVA method for assessment of the 
minimum remaining thickness of tubes in industrial heat 
exchangers, including air-cooled heat exchangers and shell-
and-tube heat exchangers.  The EVA method is built on the 
EVA procedure outlined in ASTM E-2283 – 03 by treating the 
maximum wall loss occurring on tubes as the maximum 
inclusion size in steels.  The minimum remaining thickness is 
determined simply by subtracting the calculated maximum 
wall loss from the original thickness.  The method has been 
further developed in this work to include EVA calculations for 
all the most commonly used confidence levels (viz. 99%, 
95%, 90% and 80%) at any sample size, going beyond the 
limits of a fixed confidence level at 95% and fixed sample size 
at 24 in the E-2283 – 03 procedure.   
  
Five test cases are used to evaluate the effectiveness of the 
EVA method developed in this work.  In each case, real 
inspection data from heat exchangers in petroleum refinery 
and petrochemical plant are analyzed, and the results 
presented and discussed.   
 
The effectiveness of the improved EVA method is evaluated 
per the API Risk-Based Inspection (RBI) Inspection 
Effectiveness definition [3], so that the EVA method and 
results can be easily incorporated in API RBI assessment.  
 
Finally, recommendations are given on the sample size and the 
confidence interval that may be used to achieve each level of 
API RBI inspection effectiveness.

In addition, small samples are more likely to be non-
representative of the entire heat exchanger with the sampled 
thickness values that may be all too large, too small, or too 
widespread.  When the thickness values of the sampled tubes 
are all too large, the estimated minimum thickness of the heat 
exchanger would also be large, giving a non-conservative 
estimate.  By the same token, when the thickness values of the 
sampled tubes are all very small, the estimated minimum 
thickness of the heat exchanger would be small and very 
conservative.  When the thickness values are too widespread, 
the standard deviation of the sample set would be large, 
leading to a large scale parameter, δ.  This, in turn, would lead 
to a high extreme value on the amount of wall loss, as shown 
in Equation (10), and a large confidence internal, as shown in 
Equations (11), (15) and (16).  Both of them would decrease 
the lower bound value of the minimum remaining thickness.  
The estimated minimum thickness values from all those non-
representative samples can deviate quite far from the true 
minimum and thus add to the range of data scattering.

INSPECTION EFFECTIVENESS 
 
The effectiveness of using a sampling inspection to assess the 
minimum thickness of all the tubes in a heat exchanger may be 
quantified in accordance with the Inspection Effectiveness 
definition provided in API 581 Risk-Based Inspection (RBI) 
Base Resource Document [3].   In line with the API definition, 
an inspection effectiveness may be calculated as “the 
likelihood of actual damage state not worse than estimated 
damage”.   
 
In this work, the inspection effectives is simply calculated as 
the percentage of samples giving estimated minimum 
thickness values less than or equal to the true minimum.  For 
example, if 20 samples are taken, and 15 of them have their 
estimated minimum thickness values less than or equal to the 
true minimum and the other 5 have their estimated minimum 
values greater than the true minimum, then the inspection 
effectives is equal to 15/20 = 75%.      
 
Taking the above one step further, we can use the inspection 
effectiveness levels defined in API RBI to classify the EVA 
effectiveness, so that the EVA results can be readily 
implemented in API RBI assessment.  There are five levels of 
inspection effectiveness, A through E, defined in API RBI, 
with A being the most effective and E the least. 
Quantitatively, any inspection effectiveness between 80% and 
100% is at the A level, any inspection effectiveness between 
60% and 80% is at the B level, and the same 20% range for 
each level applies all the way down to the E level, which 
covers 0% – 20% inspection effectiveness.               
 
Many people have used sampling inspections without EVA to 
assess the minimum tube thickness of tubes in their heat 
exchangers.  One common practice is to simply take the 
measured minimum value from the sampled tubes as the 
minimum thickness of all the tubes in the heat exchanger.

SUMMARY AND CONCLUDING REMARKS 
 
This paper presents an EVA method for calculation of the 
minimum tube thickness in heat exchangers.  The method is 
tested on five cases using real-world inspection data from air-
cooled and shell-and-tube heat exchangers.  These test cases 
cover various corrosion and thickness distributions.  The 
number of tubes in the exchangers ranges from 138 tubes to 
5749 tubes.  The EVA analysis results are presented and 
discussed.  The effectiveness of the EVA method is then 
evaluated per the API RBI Inspection Effectiveness definition.     
 
As discussed in the case study and inspection effectiveness 
sections of this paper, the most ideal sample size appears to be 
20 – 30 tubes, regardless of the number of tubes in the heat 
exchanger.  At that sample size, A, B, C and D levels of API 
RBI inspection effectiveness can be achieved by using the 
lower bound value of the 99%, 95%, 90%, and 80% 
confidence intervals, respectively.   
 
Note that larger sample sizes don’t necessarily mean higher 
levels of inspection effectiveness.  Even though in four of the 
five cases studied here, the inspection effectiveness increases 
with the increase of sample size, the opposite happens in the 
other one case.  This “anomaly” happens on a heat exchanger 
that has its thickness data distribution “peaked” on the right 
(high thickness value) and substantially skewed to the left 
(low thickness value).  For reasons discussed previously, the 
EVA method in such a case can generates non-conservative 
estimates (i.e. estimated minimum thickness greater than the 
true minimum), and the inspection effectiveness of using EVA 
lower bound values can actually decrease with the increase of 
sample size.  Since it’s difficult to know for sure whether such 
a thickness distribution exists without inspecting all the tubes 
(in which case EVA would not be needed), it is safer to use a 
smaller sample size, which in turn would give a larger 
confidence interval and a more conservative lower bound 
estimate.   
       
However, smaller sample sizes have their own problems too.  
As shown in this paper, the data scattering increases with the 
decrease of sample size.  At very small sample size, the spread 
can be so wide that the EVA estimates may be non-
conservative sometimes and overly conservative some other 
times.  That is, it would become difficult to get consistent, 
reliable results from the EVA method.
